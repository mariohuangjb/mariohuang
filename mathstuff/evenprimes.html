The Existence of Infinitely Many Even Primes: A "Proof"

Knowledge about elementary properties of divisibility and prime numbers is assumed in this article.

Let's recall (or learn) a famous theorem of Euclid and his proof.

Theorem. There are infintely many primes.

Proof:

  • Suppose there are only finitely many primes. Call them $p_1$, $p_2$, ..., $p_n$.
  • Consider the number $Q:=(p_1 \cdot p_2 \cdot \ldots \cdot p_n) + 1$.
  • By prime factorization, there exists a prime $p$ dividing $Q$.
  • The prime $p$ does not divide $p_1 \cdot p_2 \cdot \ldots \cdot p_n$, since otherwise $p$ would divide the difference $Q - p_1 \cdot p_2 \cdot \ldots \cdot p_n$, which is 1, but $p$ being a prime cannot divide 1.
  • So $p$ is not any prime on the list $p_1$, $p_2$, ..., $p_n$, since otherwise it would divide their product.
  • So we found a prime that is not on the list of all primes, which is a contradiction.
  • Therefore, it is impossible that there are only finitely many primes.

By modifying Euclid's proof a little bit, we can show a stronger statement: there are infinitely many primes that are even!

"Theorem". There are infintely many even primes.

Proof:

  • Suppose there are only finitely many even primes. Let $p_1$, $p_2$, ..., $p_n$ denote all the even primes that are not equal to 2.
  • Consider the number $Q:=(p_1 \cdot p_2 \cdot \ldots \cdot p_n) + 2$.
  • Being a product of even numbers, $p_1 \cdot p_2 \cdot \ldots \cdot p_n$ must be even. So $Q$ must also be even.
  • Hence, in the prime factorization of $Q$, one of the primes must be even, say $p$.
  • If $p=2$, then since $p$ divides $Q$, it follows that $p$ must also divide $Q-p$, which is $Q-2=p_1 \cdot p_2 \cdot \ldots \cdot p_n$. This would mean that $p=2$ is one of the primes $p_1$, $p_2$, ..., $p_n$, which is a contradiction since all these primes are greater than 2. Hence $p \ne 2$.
  • If $p$ is one of the primes $p_1$, $p_2$, ..., $p_n$, then $p$ would divide their product, and so it would divide $Q-p_1 \cdot p_2 \cdot \ldots \cdot p_n$, which is 2, and this is impossible because $p>2$. Hence $p$ cannot be one of $p_1$, $p_2$, ..., $p_n$.
  • So $p$ is an even prime that is not on the list of all even primes, which is a contradiction.
  • Therefore, it is impossible that there are only finitely many even primes.

Of course, as we all know, this second "theorem" above is false. But I will leave finding the fallacy in the "proof" to you.
