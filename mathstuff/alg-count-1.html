Counting Using Algebra (I)

Last time, we talked about counting integer compositions using one-to-one correspondence. If we are to count integer compositions with certain restrictions, it may not easy to find a suitable correspondence. Here we discuss a different way to count. Consider the following problem.

Given a nonnegative integer $n$, how many compositions of $n$ with all parts being $1$ or $2$ are there?

For example, when $n=5$, all such compositions are

(1,1,1,1,1)    (2,1,1,1)    (1,2,1,1)    (1,1,2,1)    (1,1,1,2)    (2,2,1)    (2,1,2)    (1,2,2),

and there are 8 of them. In particular, there are 3 of them with exactly three parts.
